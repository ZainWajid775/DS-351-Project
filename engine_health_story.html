Chapter 01 / Dataset Overview

The Fleet at a Glance

Six sensor streams. One classification. Over 19 thousand moments captured. The dataset reveals a fleet under stress — more than 1 in 3 engines show signs of fault.

⚙️

Engine RPM

Rotational speed of the engine. Faulty engines run, on average, 20% faster than healthy ones — a key early warning signal.

🛢️

Lub Oil Pressure

Lubrication oil pressure in bar. Critical for reducing friction. Healthy engines maintain optimal pressure ranges more consistently.

⛽

Fuel Pressure

Fuel delivery pressure. Wide variance in both groups but healthy engines show higher average fuel pressure (6.9 vs 6.2 bar).

💧

Coolant Pressure

Cooling system pressure. Extreme outliers in either direction often precede engine faults in the historical data.

🌡️

Lub Oil Temp

Temperature of the lubricating oil. Faulty engines run slightly hotter on average (78°C vs 77.4°C for healthy).

🌡️

Coolant Temp

Coolant temperature. Healthy engines show higher average coolant temp (78.2°C), though both groups overlap significantly.

Chapter 02 / Scrollytelling

RPM: The Loudest Signal

As you scroll through this chapter, watch the distribution shift — revealing how engine speed behaves differently across health states.

Step 1

All Engines, All Data

Here are all 19,535 engine readings plotted by RPM. The distribution spans from below 400 to over 2,000 RPM — a wide operational range. At first glance, it looks like a single, relatively normal distribution.

Healthy

Healthy Engines Peak Earlier

Isolating healthy engines, the distribution peaks around 700–750 RPM. They tend to operate at lower, steadier rotational speeds — a hallmark of well-maintained machinery running within design limits.

Faulty

Faulty Engines Run Hotter & Faster

Faulty engines show a distribution shifted right — peaking near 900–950 RPM on average. Stress, wear, and compensatory mechanisms can cause engines to spin harder. The mean difference: +148 RPM for faulty units.

Overlap

Separation Is Subtle, Not Absolute

Overlaid together, the two distributions share significant overlap. This is why RPM alone cannot diagnose a fault — but combined with other sensor signals, it becomes a powerful discriminating feature.

Chapter 05 / Findings & Conclusions

What the Data Tells Us

After analyzing 19,535 engine readings across six sensor dimensions, three clear patterns emerged.

01

RPM Is the Strongest Discriminator

Faulty engines operate at significantly higher RPM (mean 885 vs 736). This 20% elevation likely reflects compensatory mechanisms or underlying mechanical stress.

02

Fuel Pressure Drops in Fault Conditions

Healthy engines show higher average fuel pressure (6.9 vs 6.2 bar). Low fuel pressure may indicate delivery failure, clogging, or pump wear — a compounding fault factor.

03

Multi-Sensor Fusion Is Essential

No single sensor cleanly separates healthy from faulty engines. The most predictive insight comes from the combination of signals — pointing to the need for multi-variable anomaly detection.
